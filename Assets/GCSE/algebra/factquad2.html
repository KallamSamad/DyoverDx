Definitions

  • Factorising: the opposite of expanding; it means "putting into brackets".
  • Factors: parts of a number that divide exactly into it. For example, 5 divides into 10.
  • Quadratic - an expression which has a degree of 2
  • Degree- the highest power of a variable in an expression

Note that this is only for "higher" students.

Factorising \(ax^2+bx+c\)

Now, here comes a big issue. You cannot factorise these quadratics like how you do when a=1, on its own.

Have no fear! You've come too far now to give up. These quadratics require you to apply everything you've learned about expressions.

AC method

  1. Check if you can divide the whole expression to simplify
  2. Multiply a by c
  3. Find the factors of ac
  4. Rewrite in the form \(ax^2+px+qx+c\) where \(p+q=c\)
  5. Factorise in parts

Example

Factorise \(4x² + 25x + 36\)

  1. Can't simplify any further by dividing
  2. A x C = 144
  3. Factors of 144 - (1, 144), (2, 72), (3, 48), (4, 36), (6, 24), (8, 18), (9, 16) and (12, 12). Find a pair that sums to give b (25).
  4. (9,16) is the pair. Now write as \(4x^2+16x+9x+36\). Make sure you put the correct numbers next to each other. For example you could do \(4x^2+9x+16x+36\) but you know that you can't 4 and 9 doesn't have a common factor greater than 1.
  5. Factorise in parts by finding the highest common factor. You do this by factorising in pairs. So the HCF of \(4x^2+16x\) is \((4x(x+4)\) and \((9x+36\)) is \(9(x+4)\)
  6. Rewrite as \(4x(x+4)\)+\(9(x+4)\). Finally you but the numbers on the outisde in their own brackets. So the final answer is \((4x + 9)(x + 4)\)

Example 2

Factorise: \(36x² + 36x + 9\)

  • Can divide by 9 to get \(4x² + 4x + 1\) - this has saved so much time because numbers are smaller and easier
  • A x C = 4
  • Factors of 4 - (1, 4), (2, 2) Find a pair that sums to give the new c (4).
  • (2,2 is the pair. Now write as \(4x^2+2x+2x+1\).
  • Factorise in parts. \(4x^2+2x\)=\(2x(2x+1)\) and \(2x+1\) = \(2x+1\)
  • Rewrite as \(2x(2x+1)\)+\(2x+1\) = \((2x+1)(2x+1)\)

  • Finally add the 9 you factored out into the answer = \9((2x+1)(2x+1)\) because remember this rule: whenver you divide an expression it doesn't disappear - it always becomes factorised
